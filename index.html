What is the Busy Beaver function?

A Turing machine is the simplest possible computer: a tape of 0s and 1s, a head that reads and writes one cell at a time, and a small set of rules. BB(n) asks: of all n-state machines that eventually halt, which one writes the most 1s?

The answer grows so fast that no algorithm can ever compute it. BB(1) = 1. BB(2) = 4. BB(3) = 6. BB(4) = 13. Then BB(5) = 47,176,870 steps, producing 4,098 ones. BB(6) is unknown and may be unknowable within standard mathematics.

The 5/3 Ratio: How BB(5) Actually Works

We discovered that the BB(5) champion doesn't build ones monotonically. It follows a crash-rebuild sawtooth pattern governed by a single ratio: 5/3.

The Mechanism

The machine spends 99.6% of its time in just two transitions — sweeping right (state B) and sweeping left (state D). States A, C, and E fire less than 0.4% of the time, but they control everything.

Normal: Building

Sweepers bounce back and forth, extending the ones region by +2 at each boundary. Zero erasure.

Crash: ACE Erasure Chain

At a macro-boundary, states A, C, E form an erasure cascade: =A -C -E =A -C -E ... Each cycle erases 2 ones. The chain propagates until A hits a 0.

Recovery: Pure Rebuild

After the crash, the machine rebuilds with literally 0% erasure. The new peak is 5/3x higher.

The Tape Pattern

The erasure chain leaves a signature: 100100100... repeated inside the ones region. This pattern acts as a counter register — more 100 blocks means longer until the next crash. Each crash adds more blocks, which is why the intervals grow geometrically.

Research Findings

Motif Taxonomy

All BB champions share three structural motifs: sweepers (self-loops that march through 1s), gap-makers (transitions that write 0, creating internal structure), and reluctant halters (halt only triggers on a hard-to-reach condition).

Amplification Predicts Performance

We enumerated all 16.7 million 3-state machines. Only 2.5% show repeating amplification cycles, but they produce 1.7x more ones on average. 100% of machines reaching the maximum score have amplification. Deeper nesting = better: depth-2 machines score 3.1x higher than depth-0.

Oscillator vs Counter

Not all crashing machines are productive. Oscillators crash at constant intervals and never escape their cycle. Counters (like BB(5)) crash at geometrically growing intervals, doing exponentially more work each time. This distinction is a novel classification heuristic.

BB(6) Holdout Analysis

We applied our framework to all 1,214 undecided BB(6) machines from bbchallenge.org. Found 272 translated cyclers (definitively non-halting), and 0 machines with BB(5)-like counter dynamics among 500M-step runs. The counter mechanism appears to be extraordinarily rare.

The first ~35% of execution is deeply ambiguous — machines that take identical first steps can end up with any score from 0 to 6. Divergence happens at 40-60% of runtime. This quantitatively demonstrates why early filtering cannot solve the halting problem.

Champion Fragility

We systematically mutated every transition in the BB(5) champion (8,320 single-transition mutations). Every mutation either produces a trivial +1 extension or completely destroys the counter mechanism, creating oscillators instead. The champion is extraordinarily isolated in machine space — structural search cannot find counter-like machines even when starting from the champion itself.

5/3 is Novel

A literature search confirms that no prior analysis of BB(5)'s runtime dynamics has been published. The crash pattern, the 5/3 ratio, the boundary event mechanism, and the 100100100 tape pattern are all new findings.
